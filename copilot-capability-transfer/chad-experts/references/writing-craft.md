# Writing Craft — Professional Disciplines

Expert perspectives for sentence-level clarity, paragraph coherence, and prose quality. Applies to ANY extended prose production — documentation, design specs, stakeholder communications, methodology descriptions, insight files, briefings.

*Distinct from documentation-design.md (document structure) and content-design.md (content strategy). This file covers the sentence and paragraph level — how to write clear, effective prose regardless of document type.*

---

## George Gopen & Judith Swan — Reader Expectation Theory

**Source:** "The Science of Scientific Writing" (*American Scientist*, 1990); Gopen, *Expectations: Teaching Writing from the Reader's Perspective* (2004)

### Core Principles
1. **Topic position** — the beginning of a sentence establishes context. Put familiar or connecting information here.
2. **Stress position** — the end of a sentence receives natural emphasis. Put the new, important information here.
3. **Old before new** — readers expect each sentence to begin with information they already know, then move to new information. Violating this forces re-reading.
4. **One story per sentence** — a sentence should make one point. When it tries to make two, readers may not grasp either.
5. **Subject-verb proximity** — readers expect the verb shortly after the subject. Separating them with long interrupting clauses forces the reader to hold too much in working memory.
6. **Whose story?** — the grammatical subject of the sentence tells the reader whose story is being told. Mismatch between the actual actor and the grammatical subject disorients readers.

### Application Checklist
- [ ] Read the first and last sentence of each paragraph — does the first establish context and the last deliver the point?
- [ ] Are the grammatical subjects real actors? ("The team decided" not "A decision was made")
- [ ] Spot-check 2-3 key sentences: is the most important information at the end (stress position)?

## Joseph Williams — Style: Clarity and Grace

**Source:** *Style: Lessons in Clarity and Grace* (1981; 13th ed. 2023 with Joseph Bizup)

### Core Principles
1. **Characters as subjects** — make the main characters of your story the grammatical subjects of your sentences. Readers understand prose best when the subjects name the cast of characters.
2. **Actions as verbs** — express key actions as verbs, not nominalizations. "We analyzed the data" not "An analysis of the data was performed."
3. **Nominalizations are the enemy** — turning verbs into nouns ("implementation" instead of "implement," "utilization" instead of "use") makes prose abstract and hard to follow.
4. **Cohesion and coherence** — cohesion links each sentence to the previous one (old-new flow). Coherence gives the paragraph an overall point that each sentence serves.
5. **Short-to-long** — within a sentence, put shorter, simpler elements before longer, complex ones. This reduces cognitive load.
6. **Concision** — cut meaningless words, redundant pairs, obvious implications, and empty hedges. "In order to" → "to." "Due to the fact that" → "because."

### Application Checklist
- [ ] Scan for nominalizations (-tion, -ment, -ness, -ity endings) — are key actions buried in nouns instead of expressed as verbs?
- [ ] Does each paragraph have a clear point sentence that the other sentences serve?
- [ ] Can any redundant pairs, empty hedges, or filler phrases be cut?

## Larry McEnerney — Writing as Value Creation

**Source:** University of Chicago Writing Program; "The Craft of Writing Effectively" (Leadership Lab lecture, widely cited)

### Core Principles
1. **Writing is not thinking on paper** — good thinking does not automatically produce good writing. Writing must be designed for readers, not as an externalization of the writer's thought process.
2. **Value, not knowledge** — readers don't read to learn what you know. They read because your writing helps them do something, decide something, or understand something that matters to them. Writing must create VALUE for the reader.
3. **Problem before solution** — readers engage when you establish a problem they care about. Starting with your solution (answer, framework, methodology) gives them no reason to care. Establish the cost of the status quo first.
4. **Instability as engine** — effective writing opens with instability: tension, contradiction, gap, challenge. The reader keeps reading to resolve the instability. No instability = no reason to continue.
5. **Community expectations** — every professional community has conventions about what counts as evidence, how arguments are structured, and what "good" looks like. Effective writing operates within these conventions (even when challenging them).
6. **The function of every sentence** — every sentence should do one of: establish the problem, present evidence, draw a conclusion, or signal structure. Sentences that don't serve a function are clutter.

### Application Checklist
- [ ] Does the opening establish a problem the reader cares about before presenting the solution?
- [ ] Would the reader say "so what?" at any point? (value test)
- [ ] Does every paragraph advance the argument, or is any section just expressing what the writer knows?

## Steven Pinker — The Sense of Style

**Source:** *The Sense of Style: The Thinking Person's Guide to Writing in the 21st Century* (2014)

### Core Principles
1. **Classic style** — the writer sees something, turns to the reader, and points to it. The writer's job is to help the reader see what the writer sees. Prose is a window, not a mirror.
2. **The curse of knowledge** — the single biggest barrier to clear writing. Once you know something, you can't imagine what it's like not to know it. You omit explanations, use jargon, and create logical gaps that are invisible to you.
3. **Arcs of coherence** — readers need to know where they are in the argument at every moment. Coherence comes from making the structure visible: not just what's being said, but how it relates to what came before and what comes next.
4. **Tree structures, not strings** — sentences and paragraphs are trees (hierarchical), not strings (linear). Readers parse them by finding the main branch (subject-verb) and hanging subordinate branches off it. Confusing sentences have ambiguous tree structures.
5. **Show, don't just tell** — concrete examples, specific details, and visual language make abstract ideas accessible. Abstractions without examples are empty; examples without abstractions are anecdotes.

### Application Checklist
- [ ] Would someone without your context understand this without re-reading? (curse of knowledge)
- [ ] Are abstract ideas grounded with at least one concrete example?
- [ ] Is the reader's position in the argument clear at every section break? (coherence arcs)

## William Zinsser — On Writing Well

**Source:** *On Writing Well* (1976; 8th ed. 2006)

### Core Principles
1. **Simplicity** — clear thinking becomes clear writing. If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it well enough. Strip every sentence to its cleanest components.
2. **Clutter** — the disease of writing. Every long word that could be short, every adverb that carries the same meaning as the verb, every passive construction that leaves out who is doing what — it's clutter. Cut it.
3. **The audience** — you are writing for yourself. If you try to write for a "general audience," you write for nobody. Write to express what interests you, and you'll find your readers.
4. **Unity** — every piece of writing should have unity of pronoun (first person? third?), tense (past? present?), mood (formal? casual?), and scope. Mixing these disorients the reader.
5. **Words** — use the first word that comes to mind if it is appropriate and colorful. Don't use a long word when a short one works. Don't use "implement" when you mean "do." Don't use "utilize" when you mean "use."

### Application Checklist
- [ ] Read one paragraph aloud — can any words be cut without losing meaning?
- [ ] Is there unity of pronoun, tense, and mood throughout the piece?
- [ ] Scan for "utilize," "implement," "leverage" — are simpler words available?

## Cross-Reference: Problem → Framework

| Writing Problem | Primary Framework(s) |
|----------------|---------------------|
| Sentences are confusing on first read | Gopen & Swan (topic/stress positions, old-before-new) |
| Prose feels abstract or bureaucratic | Williams (nominalizations → verbs, characters → subjects) |
| Reader doesn't engage or care | McEnerney (problem before solution, instability as engine, value for reader) |
| Writer assumes too much reader knowledge | Pinker (curse of knowledge, classic style) |
| Text is bloated / overwritten | Zinsser (simplicity, clutter, cut ruthlessly) |
| Paragraphs lack coherence | Williams (point sentence + supporting sentences), Gopen & Swan (old-before-new flow) |
| Technical writing feels like a lecture | Pinker (classic style — point to something, don't perform expertise), McEnerney (value, not knowledge) |
