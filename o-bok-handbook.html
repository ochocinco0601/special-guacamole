Business Observability

The Observability Body of Knowledge (O-BOK) — a practitioner handbook

Observability begins with defining success in business health terms — not with telemetry.

Traditional monitoring starts with the data you can collect — metrics, logs, traces — and layers business meaning on top when time permits. Business observability reverses that order. Business context is defined first. Signals are designed to measure that context. Everything downstream — dashboards, alerts, playbooks — derives from the business definition.

This is a minority position in the observability industry, and it is deliberate. A telemetry-first practice produces comprehensive dashboards that an executive cannot use to answer "is the business healthy right now?" A business-context-first practice can.

The paradigm distinction: SLO/SLI practice (Google SRE) is compatible — an SLO is a quantified stakeholder expectation. Business observability extends SRE with the business-context layer that SLOs alone don't require. Teams with mature SLO practice have most of Layer 3. What's typically missing is the structured stakeholder-expectation and business-impact surface around them.

The Four-Layer Model

Observability signals organize into four layers. The first two are traditional monitoring. The second two are the business observability contribution — the business context layer most organizations lack.

LayerNameQuestionOwner
L1 System Is the platform operational? SRE / Platform
L2 Process Is the workflow executing correctly? Developer / QA
L3 Business Health Is the stakeholder expectation being met? Product Owner
L4 Business Impact How bad is it when we fail? PO + Ops / Compliance

Layers 1–2 validate the mechanism (up, fast, correct). Layer 3 validates the intended outcome. Layer 4 quantifies the consequence when outcomes miss.

A service can be green on L1–L2 (infrastructure healthy, workflows running) while failing on L3 (business outcomes not met). That gap is why L3/L4 exist.

The Semantic Chain

Every signal traces back to a business reason through an explicit chain:

[Stakeholder] has an [Expectation] → measured by a [Business Health Signal L3] with a target and formula → supported by [Process Signals L2] and [System Signals L1] → if breached: [Business Impact L4]: category + magnitude → [Alert] → [Owner + Next Action]

No signal exists without a stakeholder expectation it serves. No stakeholder expectation exists without a signal measuring it. The chain is enforced structurally — traceability is a data-model relationship, not a documentation convention.

Define — Business Context & Service Definition

Owner: Product Owner  |  Question: What does "healthy" mean in business terms?

What the PO produces

A service profile — the root entity that captures everything needed to make a service observable in business terms:

ComponentWhat it captures
Service identityName, business purpose, tier (1–6), org placement, CMDB (Configuration Management Database) app ID
Stakeholder expectationsWho cares and what they need (3–5 rows per service, forced prioritization)
Health signals L3What "working" looks like, quantified as SLOs with targets and time windows
Impact signals L4What failure costs across four impact categories
TraceabilityEvery signal → stakeholder expectation it serves
Operational readinessDashboard URL, runbook URL, alert targets, escalation policy

Stakeholder expectations

Each expectation reads as a complete story: "As [stakeholder], I expect [X]. If broken: [impact]."

FieldExample
StakeholderMortgage applicants (specific, not "customers")
ExpectationWire transfers complete on scheduled closing date
Impact categorycustomer_experience, financial, legal_risk, or operational
Impact descriptionCustomer Impact: Homebuyers cannot complete purchase on scheduled date
PriorityCRITICAL / HIGH / MEDIUM / LOW

SLOs

SLOs translate stakeholder expectations into quantitative targets. An SLO has a target (e.g., 99.5%), a time window (e.g., 30 days rolling), and a budgeting method (Occurrences, Timeslices, or RatioTimeslices). Error budgets make reliability trade-offs explicit — when the budget is consumed, reliability is prioritized over velocity.

The workflow

  1. Identify service — PO names the service, its business purpose, and org placement
  2. Define stakeholders — PO captures 3–5 stakeholder expectations with impact categories
  3. Define health — PO creates L3 signals with SLO targets
  4. Define impact — PO creates L4 signals quantifying failure consequences
  5. Map traceability — PO links each signal to the stakeholder expectation it serves
  6. Evaluate — Service Status score shows completeness across 5 dimensions

The first profile for a service takes approximately 15 minutes with AI-assisted pre-population from existing content.

Three service definition patterns

PatternWhen to useExample
CMDB ApplicationOne app, one service"Wire Transfer Engine"
Capability within an AppOne function inside a large app"Appraisal Order Processing" within Home Lending
Business Capability spanning AppsStakeholder-meaningful process across multiple apps"Mortgage Origination" spanning credit, appraisal, underwriting, closing

Implement — Signal Design & Instrumentation

Owner: Developer  |  Question: How do we instrument the signals that matter?

Signal types by layer

LayerSignal TypesMeasurement Pattern
L1 Systemsli_ratio, sli_thresholdInfrastructure: uptime %, latency, CPU, memory
L2 Processsli_ratio, sli_thresholdApplication: validation rates, batch completion, error rates
L3 Business Healthsli_ratio, sli_thresholdOutcomes: fill rate %, pass rate %, on-time rate
L4 Business Impactbusiness_impactConsequence: customers_affected, dollars_at_risk, compliance_cases

Two measurement types for L1–L3:

  • sli_ratio — good events / total events. "99.5% of page loads complete in < 2 seconds."
  • sli_threshold — metric vs. absolute limit. "Average response time stays below 500ms."

L4 is structurally different: impact signals don't have SLO targets. They activate when an L3 signal breaches its target, quantifying the consequence. Event-driven, not continuously measured.

Three-actor ownership model

ActorContributes
Product OwnerSignal definition — name, business description, SLO target
DeveloperImplementation — signal type, queries, data source
Platform EngineerConfiguration — alert thresholds, severity, routing

A single signal record accumulates fields from all three actors over time. The PO can create a signal before the Developer has written any queries — business context should not be blocked by technical implementation.

Instrumentation patterns

  1. Code-level — signals emitted from application code (counters, gauges, histograms). Most L2 and L3 signals.
  2. Pipeline — signals derived from data processing (batch completion rates, queue depths, ETL success).
  3. Agent-based — external observers (infrastructure metrics, synthetic monitoring, log-based extraction).

Enforce — Standards & Quality

Owner: Platform / Engineering  |  Question: How do we ensure standards before production?

Service Status evaluation

The primary quality gate — a completeness score (0–100) computed across five dimensions:

DimensionWeightWhat it measures
Layer Coverage20%Signals across all 4 layers?
Signal Completeness30%Required fields filled for each signal?
Threshold Coverage15%Health signals have alert/page thresholds?
Traceability Coverage20%Every stakeholder has at least one linked signal?
Operational Readiness15%Dashboard, runbook, alert targets, escalation defined?

Computed on every access, never stored. Progressive — a new service scores 0% and rises as the PO adds content. Teams see progress incrementally.

Production-ready means

  1. Alert notification targets — who gets notified
  2. Dashboard URL — where the team sees service health
  3. Runbook URL — the documented response procedure
  4. Escalation policy — who to contact when first responder can't resolve

Observe — Monitoring, Visualization & Correlation

Owner: Platform / SRE  |  Question: What views make health visible?

Dashboard hierarchy

LevelAudienceQuestion
Product LinesExecutiveIs anything red?
ProductsDirectorWhich product area needs attention?
ServicesManager / POWhich service is degraded?
Service DetailPO / Dev / SREWhat's happening with this service?
Signal DetailDev / SREWhat's the signal showing?

Navigation is drill-down: each click narrows focus from portfolio to signal.

Business-first display order

At the Service Detail level, information is sequenced by what practitioners actually have:

  1. Stakeholder expectations — always exist (PO provides). Who cares and what they need.
  2. Operational signals — the actual diagnostic truth. What's the current health?
  3. Business impact estimates — reference context with uncertainty language ("~", "Estimated Impact").
  4. SLI — the goal. May be blank in Year 1 for many services.

Signal trust hierarchy

  • SLI (highest) — actual measurement of service health. This IS what's happening.
  • Operational signals (high) — component health. These components ARE functioning/failing.
  • Business impact estimates (lower) — calculated from assumptions. Directionally useful, not exact.

Respond — Incident Management

Owner: SRE / Ops  |  Question: When something fails, what do we do?

Business-context-aware triage

  1. Which layers are affected? L3 breach with L1/L2 green = business logic problem. L1 breach cascading to L3 = infrastructure problem with business impact.
  2. What's the business impact? L4 signals quantify: customers affected, revenue at risk, compliance exposure.
  3. Who's the stakeholder? Stakeholder expectations tell you who cares. CRITICAL regulatory expectation > MEDIUM internal expectation.

Escalation by layer

Affected LayerFirst ResponderEscalation Path
L1 SystemPlatform / SREInfrastructure → Platform lead
L2 ProcessDeveloper on-callApp team → Dev lead → Architecture
L3 Business HealthPO + Dev on-callBusiness escalation + technical investigation
L4 Business ImpactIncident commanderCross-functional: PO + Dev + SRE + leadership

Playbooks are pre-built response procedures tied to signal patterns. A playbook answers: "This alert fired. Now what?"

The feedback loop: every incident is a business context audit. Did we know the affected stakeholders? Were impact categories correct? Was the right person paged? Gaps feed back to Define.

The Onboarding Pipeline

The methodology becomes operational through a pipeline that takes a service from "no business observability" to "deployed dashboard with live telemetry."

Sources (docs, code, ops history) → Capture (structured understanding about the service) ← requires judgment → Validate (quality gate — completeness, citations) ← mechanical → Load (managed service profile in the data model) ← mechanical → Factory (profile → dashboard JSON) ← mechanical → Deploy (live dashboard querying real datasources) ← mechanical

One judgment step, five mechanical steps

Capture is the hard part. Reading source material about a service and producing a structured understanding of who depends on it, what "healthy" means, what breaks when it fails, and what signals measure those expectations. This requires judgment — deciding which stakeholders matter, designing signals at the right layer, setting SLO targets with rationale.

Everything after capture is mechanical. Validate checks the structure. Load transforms it into the data model. Factory produces artifacts. Deploy puts them live. These steps don't make decisions — they execute deterministic transforms on the understanding that capture produced.

The Service Understanding Document

The capture step produces a Service Understanding Document (SUD) — a structured record of everything known about a service's business context, stakeholders, signals, and operational readiness. It's produced by answering 51 questions organized by what they capture:

SectionQuestionsWhat it captures
What is this service?10Identity, purpose, tier, ownership, dependencies, failure modes
Who cares?5Stakeholders, expectations, impact, priority
What signals?12 (per signal)Layer, type, SLO target, ownership, causal relationships
What impact?6Impact metrics, affected populations, financial exposure
Traceability4Signal → stakeholder mappings, causal chains between layers
Operational8Dashboard, runbook, alert targets, escalation, dependencies
Emerging6Understanding that runs ahead of what the data model can express today

Every answer requires source citations and reasoning. When someone six months later asks "why is the SLO target 95%?", the citation chain traces back to the specific document or conversation that justified it.

Not all questions will have answers — real sources are messy and incomplete. The SUD captures understanding as it exists now. Fields populate later as better sources become available.

What the factories produce

The service profile in the data model is the stable contract. Everything upstream produces it; everything downstream consumes it. Currently operational:

FactoryInputOutput
Dashboard FactoryService profileGrafana dashboard JSON — panels organized by business health → impact → process → system

The same data model feeds alert configs, runbooks, playbooks, and signal specs as those factories are built. One PO action → multiple automated outputs.

Why the SUD is the durable asset: Dashboards, alerts, and playbooks are derived outputs that factories regenerate whenever the factory logic improves. If a factory gets a better layout algorithm, you re-run it against the same profile. You don't re-capture the understanding.

Enabling Capabilities

Platform & Tooling

Question: What technology makes observability possible?

Covers tool selection, telemetry pipeline architecture, consolidation strategy, observability economics. Business observability abstracts over tool choice through a data_source field — a signal can come from any supported platform (Splunk, Grafana, Dynatrace, etc.).

Industry research (11 maturity models from AWS, Grafana, Splunk, New Relic, Honeycomb, Dynatrace, DORA) shows convergence on a common arc: reactive → proactive → predictive. The industry is stuck at the transition from technical observability to business-aware — only 15–20% of organizations have crossed it.

Integration & Architecture

Question: How does observability connect to enterprise systems?

The CMDB application ID is the primary join key between the observability catalog and enterprise systems. Every service entry ties to a CMDB entry.

The CMDB granularity problem: A single CMDB app ID often contains 50–100 separate repositories spanning multiple business capabilities. Business observability addresses this through service definition pattern #2 — defining health at the capability level even when the CMDB entry is monolithic. The observability service registry complements the CMDB, not duplicates it.

Integration surfaces: CMDB/ServiceNow, CI/CD pipelines (quality gate as deployment gate), regulatory frameworks, enterprise architecture (BIAN, ITIL, TOGAF).

Data Governance

Question: How do we maintain data coherence at scale?

The service catalog is the system-of-record — 15+ fields per service capturing business context that otherwise exists only in people's heads. At 20 services × 3–4 contexts each = 60–80 records of organizational knowledge made explicit.

Naming discipline: Machine-friendly (signal_name) + human-readable (signal_display_name). Four fixed impact categories. Avoid shorthand that collides across contexts.

Currency problem: Catalogs go stale. Two feedback loops: reactive (postmortem finds gaps → update profile) + proactive (scheduled reviews triggered by org change). Audit trail (modified_at) detects staleness.

Managing the Practice

Strategy & Program Management

Question: How does observability align with enterprise direction?

A typical program uses four Key Results: methodology validation → implementation across services → cross-org adoption → enterprise integration. Each KR has quantified measures and dependencies.

Common governance structure: A champions working group — recurring meeting of regular participants from multiple LOBs and platform teams, convened by senior line leadership, co-facilitated by a practice lead. Pre-PMO structure being pushed toward more disciplined output as the practice matures.

Adoption & Enablement

Question: How do teams actually start?

Onboarding workflow (~15 min with AI pre-population)

  1. Service basics — name, purpose, tier
  2. Stakeholder expectations — who cares, what breaks (repeatable for each stakeholder)
  3. Business health signals + SLO configuration
  4. Technical signals (L1/L2)
  5. Business impact quantification
  6. Technical impact mapping
  7. Preview and validate

Two modes: Learn (explanation + examples at each step) vs. Fast (minimized guidance for experienced teams).

Adoption funnel: Awareness → First touch (onboarding) → Pilot (with friction logging) → Validation → Team adoption → Scale. Friction logging captures pain points in real time during onboarding — what didn't work, context, workaround — to be analyzed later.

Walking a Question

The handbook's distinctive value: trace any observability question through the dependency chain to find where it actually breaks.

Example: "We need better signal correlation."

1
Locate. Correlation is a monitoring activity → sits in Observe.
2
Follow upstream. Observe depends on Implement — you cannot correlate signals not designed to relate to each other.
3
Follow again. Implement depends on Define — you cannot design signals without knowing which expectations they serve.
4
Check content state at each stop. Observe: solid. Implement: thin signal-derivation content. Define: solid.
5
Read the walk. The chain breaks in the middle. Correlation infrastructure built on weak signal design can't make business sense of what it's correlating.
Investment conclusion: Fix signal design (Implement) before adding correlation capability (Observe). The walk produced a specific investment target that wasn't visible from the correlation question alone.

More walks

QuestionWalkWhere it usually breaks
"Our signals are noisy"Observe → Implement → Enforce → GovernanceStandards not enforced, or no one accountable for signal quality
"Leadership can't see business impact"Observe → DefineL3/L4 signals aren't defined — no dashboard can surface what doesn't exist
"Dashboards don't drive decisions"Observe → Stakeholders → StrategyMisaligned altitude or missing business context, not rendering choices
"We can't get teams to adopt"Adoption → Stakeholders → CapabilitySpecific point in the funnel — identify which
"Incidents reveal unknown stakeholders"Respond → DefineBusiness context currency — the profile hasn't been reviewed since last org change

The pattern

  1. Locate — find the lifecycle stage the question addresses
  2. Follow upstream — walk the dependency chain toward Define
  3. Check state — at each stop, is there real substance or a gap?
  4. Read the walk — a break is where weaker state sits upstream of stronger
  5. Name the investment target — the break is where additional work has the most leverage

Glossary

TermDefinition
Business ObservabilityMethodology that defines success in business health terms before designing signals, dashboards, or alerts. Reverses the telemetry-first order.
Four-Layer ModelSignal classification by business meaning: System (L1), Process (L2), Business Health (L3), Business Impact (L4). L1–L2 = traditional monitoring. L3–L4 = business observability contribution.
Semantic ChainThe explicit traceability from stakeholder → expectation → health signal → supporting signals → impact → alert → owner. Every signal connects back to a business reason.
SignalA quantifiable measurement of service health or business outcome. Not a synonym for "metric" — a signal has an owner, a layer, a stakeholder it serves, and a target.
Signal TypeHow a signal is measured. sli_ratio = good events / total events. sli_threshold = metric vs. absolute limit. business_impact = consequence quantification (L4 only).
SLIService Level Indicator. A quantifiable measure of service health (Layers 1–3). The measurement that an SLO sets a target for.
SLOService Level Objective. A quantified stakeholder expectation — target (e.g., 99.5%), time window (e.g., 30 days), budgeting method. In this methodology, an SLO is the Layer 3 expression of a stakeholder expectation.
Error BudgetThe allowed failure margin derived from an SLO. If SLO is 99.5% over 30 days, the error budget is 0.5% (~2h 10m). When consumed, reliability is prioritized over velocity.
Stakeholder ExpectationA specific statement of what a named stakeholder group expects from a service, with an impact category and priority. Reads as: "As [stakeholder], I expect [X]. If broken: [impact]."
Impact CategoryOne of four fixed types classifying business damage: customer_experience, financial, legal_risk, operational.
Service ProfileThe root entity in the data model. Captures a service's identity, stakeholder expectations, health signals, impact signals, traceability mappings, and operational readiness.
Service Understanding Document (SUD)The structured capture artifact — produced by answering 51 questions about a service against real sources, with citations and reasoning. The durable understanding asset that the pipeline transforms into profiles and artifacts.
Three-Actor OwnershipThe defining characteristic of signal implementation: Product Owner defines (what and why), Developer implements (how to measure), Platform Engineer configures (when to alert). One signal, three contributors.
Service StatusA computed completeness score (0–100) across five dimensions: layer coverage, signal completeness, threshold coverage, traceability coverage, operational readiness. Computed on every access, never stored.
TraceabilityThe structural requirement that every signal maps to a stakeholder expectation it serves, and every stakeholder expectation has at least one signal measuring it. Enforced in the data model, not a documentation convention.
Knowledge Area (KA)A major division of the observability domain — what practitioners need to understand. 13 KAs across three tiers: Core Practice (lifecycle), Enabling Practice (infrastructure), Management Practice (organizational).
Practice InstrumentA concrete tool a practitioner picks up and uses — template, assessment, factory, protocol. 22 instruments in five types by action: Assessment, Definition, Production, Adoption, Communication.
PerspectiveA platform-type lens that bends how Knowledge Areas and Instruments apply. Same KAs, different techniques. Initial set: Cloud-Native, Mainframe, Hybrid, SaaS.
FactoryAn automated production system that reads a service profile and generates artifacts — dashboards, alerts, playbooks, documentation, user stories. One PO action → multiple outputs.
